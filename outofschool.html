OUT OF SCHOOL LEARNING

Through after-school and holiday programmes, and projects with voluntary and statutory organisations, young people gained technical, creative and production skills. They worked in small groups and were encouraged to take responsibility for their films at every stage of production and to develop their own creative ideas for further work.

Young people living in an area where they were exposed to risk chose to go to what they called ‘the video place’ – a safe space where they could grow in confidence, gain skills, make films and form friendships beyond their existing peer groups. Many of these films were directly informed by their own experiences and concerns – bullying, relationships and feeling different.

There were regular screenings attended by family and friends, at which they introduced their work and responded to questions from the audience. The process of filmmaking requires more than technical skills, it also demands that people work as a team, communicate, manage people, propose and discuss ideas, think analytically and creatively, and negotiate shared decisions. These are core skills that they were able to use to get on at school and move into work or further education.
